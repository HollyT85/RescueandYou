Diet

Some animals may enter rescue having never been fed a proper diet. An example would be Jessie & James; two cockatiels who were relinquished to rescue.
When they arrived at their foster (which turned into a forever) home, they had rabbit muesli in their bowl. When they were given bird seed they didn't have a clue what to do with it to start with. It soon emeerged they had mainly been fed on toast and scraps of food rather than having a proper diet. The birds were despondent and lethargic until they started eating a proper diet.
Different animals can suffer different consequences of poor diet and this will be one of the first things that often needs correcting. The rescue center you rescue from will advise you every step of the way on this.

Separation Anxiety

Many animals who enter rescue suffer with separation anxiety. This is where they struggle with being left alone; sometimes for as little as one minute.
Separation anxiety can cause an animal to be destructive and noisy; they may pant, salivate and/or use the house as a toilet.
There are many ways to help a dog which suffers however, they take time and patience; there is no quick fix!

Crate Training

There is a lot of speculation that using a crate is cruel. This is NOT true. Many dogs find having a crate relaxing and comforting when they are used correctly. Crates are not a punishment tool but can be an effective way to keep dogs who are destructive safe when you leave the house.
Crates should have a comfortable bed in there (but not something that can be easily destroyed) and many people also cover them to provide an extra level of comfort. The door should be left open when the dog is not in it so they can go in and out as they feel fit; when they are used correctly, many dogs will take themselves in there when they want some space.

New Introductions

Any new rescue animal may require time to settle before being introduced to any other pets or people outside of their new home. It's natural to want to introduce them quickly however, this is not what is best for the animal.
Introductions should begin slowly; animals in the same house may require some separation to begin with especially if one is inquisitive and the other is not; this is totally fine and all part of the process. Small and steady steps that work are better than big steps all at once which often see animals returned to rescue centers quickly.

Enjoy

Whilst rescue is hard work, it is one of the most rewarding things you can do. Enjoy!
